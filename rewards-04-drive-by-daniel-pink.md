# 4. Resource Summary: Drive by Daniel Pink

## Core idea

*Drive* argues that for complex, creative, and meaningful work, people are motivated less by simple carrots and sticks and more by:

1. Autonomy
2. Mastery
3. Purpose

Money matters, especially when compensation is unfair. But once basic fairness is met, people often care deeply about trust, growth, and meaningful impact.

## Why it matters for product teams

SaaS products often serve professionals doing complex work. A reward system that feels too simplistic can backfire.

A product manager, sales leader, support agent, developer, CFO, or HR leader does not want to be treated like a child collecting stickers. They want useful feedback that makes them better.

## SaaS example: developer tool

A developer platform wants more users to configure monitoring.

Shallow reward:

- Badge: "You created 5 alerts!"

Better reward:

- "Your team is now covered for the top 3 production failure modes."
- "Mean time to detection improved by 35%."
- "You prevented 2 incidents from reaching customers this month."

This supports mastery and purpose.

## Internal product-team scenario

A SaaS company rewards product teams for shipping features. Teams ship more, but customer confusion rises.

Better internal incentives:

- Problems solved
- Customer outcomes improved
- Support tickets reduced
- Adoption quality
- Retention lift
- Reliability improvements
- Decommissioning unused features

## Questions

1. Are we using a childish reward for professional work?
2. Does the reward support mastery?
3. Does the reward connect to purpose?
4. Does it encourage teams to ship or to solve?
5. What would a high-autonomy reward system look like?

## Activity

Rewrite a product-team OKR.

Bad version:

- Objective: Increase adoption of automation.
- Key Result: 1,000 users try automation.

Better version:

- Objective: Help operations teams save time through reliable automation.
- Key Results:
  - 40% of target accounts run at least one automation weekly.
  - Users report saving at least 2 hours per week.
  - Automation failure rate stays below 1%.
  - Support tickets per automation run do not increase.

Now create your own bad and better OKR.
