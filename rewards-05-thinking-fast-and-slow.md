# 5. Resource Summary: Thinking, Fast and Slow

## Core idea

Daniel Kahneman describes two modes of thinking:

- **System 1**: fast, automatic, emotional, intuitive
- **System 2**: slow, deliberate, analytical, effortful

SaaS products often influence behavior through System 1: progress indicators, defaults, urgency, status, social proof, loss aversion, and immediate feedback.

## Product implications

Users do not evaluate product rewards purely rationally. They respond to framing.

Examples:

- "Complete setup" feels like a chore.
- "Get your first useful forecast" feels like progress.
- "Invite your team" feels like work.
- "Bring your team into one shared view" feels like value.

## SaaS example: loss aversion

A customer-success platform shows an account health score dropping from 82 to 71.

This can motivate action because users dislike losing ground. But if the score is unexplained, it can create anxiety.

Better design:

- Show why the score dropped.
- Show the top corrective action.
- Show likely impact.
- Frame the system as coaching, not punishment.

## SaaS example: goal-gradient effect

Users become more motivated as they approach a goal.

An onboarding checklist that says "80% complete" can increase completion. But only if the checklist maps to real value.

Bad checklist:

- Add profile photo
- Choose theme
- Read intro tooltip

Better checklist:

- Connect real data
- Invite one collaborator
- Create first saved report
- Schedule weekly digest

## Questions

1. What framing makes the desired behavior feel valuable?
2. Are we using loss aversion responsibly?
3. Does the progress bar represent real progress?
4. What default would most help the user?
5. What reward might be psychologically stronger than its economic value?

## Activity

Take one SaaS action and frame it five ways.

Action: Invite a teammate.

Frames:

1. Functional: "Invite a teammate."
2. Progress: "You are one step away from a shared workspace."
3. Social: "Bring your team into the same view."
4. Risk-reduction: "Avoid decisions being trapped in one person's account."
5. Competence: "Set up collaboration like a high-performing team."

Now do this for your product's key activation action.
