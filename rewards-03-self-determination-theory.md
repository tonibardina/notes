# 3. Resource Summary: Self-Determination Theory

## Core idea

Self-Determination Theory, developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan, argues that high-quality motivation depends on three psychological needs:

1. **Autonomy**: I feel agency and choice.
2. **Competence**: I feel capable and improving.
3. **Relatedness**: I feel connected to others or to meaningful work.

## Why it matters for SaaS

A SaaS reward system should ideally support at least one of these needs.

### Autonomy in SaaS

Good reward design gives users control.

Example:

- A workflow automation product lets users choose their own automation goal and then shows progress toward it.

Bad version:

- The product nags users to configure automations they do not need.

### Competence in SaaS

Good reward design helps users feel better at their job.

Example:

- An analytics tool explains: "You found a revenue anomaly 3 days earlier than usual."

Bad version:

- "You clicked 10 charts this week."

### Relatedness in SaaS

Good reward design improves team connection.

Example:

- A customer-support platform recognizes when one teammate writes an answer that helps the whole team resolve future tickets faster.

Bad version:

- A leaderboard ranks agents only by ticket volume, encouraging speed over collaboration.

## SaaS scenario

A cybersecurity compliance SaaS gives companies a security score. If the score is vague, punitive, and unexplained, admins feel judged. If the score is transparent, actionable, and improvement-oriented, it supports competence.

Better design:

- Explain the score drivers.
- Show the highest-risk gaps.
- Recommend next actions.
- Celebrate improvement, not just perfection.
- Benchmark against similar companies.

## Questions

1. Does this reward make the user feel controlled or empowered?
2. Does it help the user understand progress?
3. Does it build team trust or unhealthy comparison?
4. Does it reward mastery or mere activity?
5. What would the user say they became better at because of this system?
