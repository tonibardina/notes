# 7. Resource Summary: Predictably Irrational

## Core idea

Dan Ariely's work highlights that people make predictable mistakes. They are influenced by "free," comparison, expectations, ownership, social norms, and market norms.

## Why it matters for SaaS

SaaS users compare plans, features, limits, credits, and value in non-rational ways.

A "free" feature can feel more attractive than a more valuable but paid feature. A credit system can make usage feel less concrete. A public badge can feel motivating in one context and embarrassing in another.

## SaaS example: free tier

A free tier can reduce adoption friction, but it also shapes user perception.

It can encourage:

- Experimentation
- Product-led growth
- Team sharing
- Bottom-up adoption

It can also create:

- Low-quality usage
- Support burden
- Poor activation if value is gated
- Reluctance to pay

## SaaS example: social vs market norms

A SaaS community asks users to answer questions. If users help because they value the community, public recognition may work well. If the company starts paying small amounts per answer, the interaction may become transactional and quality may drop.

Better rewards might include:

- Expert status
- Early feature access
- Invitation to advisory councils
- Public recognition
- Influence over roadmap
- Certification

## Questions

1. Are we turning a social behavior into a transactional behavior?
2. Is "free" creating healthy exploration or low-value usage?
3. What comparison are users making when they see the reward?
4. Does the reward feel fair?
5. Does the reward change the relationship between user and company?

## Activity

Design two reward systems for a SaaS community:

1. Monetary reward system
2. Recognition-based reward system

Compare:

- Contribution quality
- Community culture
- Cost
- Fairness
- Gaming risk
- Long-term sustainability
